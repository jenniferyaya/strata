Approach

Strata is a system for reading cities through the literature they produced or that was written inside them. It works from a simple premise: that place is not background. Cities shape the conditions of thought. Streets, quarters, seasons, and the social textures of a moment all enter the written work, and the written work in turn becomes a lens through which the city is seen.

Three axes

City × Time × Perspective

City

Each city is treated as a literary site, a place with a documented relationship to writing. The selection is intentional, not exhaustive. Cities are chosen because something significant happened in them, not merely because writers passed through.

Time

Literature is mapped to the period in which it was written or set, not the year of publication. This distinction matters. A novel written in 1960 about events in 1890 belongs to 1890 in this system. The era buttons bring this to light, allowing the place to shift beneath you as time moves.

How to read the map

The map is not a timeline and it is not a guide. It is closer to an index — a record of where writing was rooted. Selecting a city opens its shelf: a set of works tied to that place and period. Clicking on the era buttons changes which works are visible. The pins on the map follow. What disappears is not erased; it is waiting at another position on the axis.

The shelf is ordered by neighbourhood, not by date or author. This is deliberate. The spatial logic of a city, its districts, their character, their distance from one another, is part of the literary meaning. Montparnasse in 1923 is not interchangeable with Saint-Germain-des-Prés in 1948, even within the same city.

Each entry carries a title, an author, a year, and a quarter. Where the work is set and where it was written are often the same location. Where they differ, the listed area reflects the primary setting of the text.

"A writer only begins a book. A reader finishes it." Samuel Johnson

Strata exists because literary geography is underbuilt as a field of public access. The scholarship is there but the maps are not. This is an attempt to make the spatial dimension of literature visible, not as annotation, and not as tourism, but as a way of understanding what cities do to the people who write inside them.
